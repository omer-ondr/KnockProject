# Knockin' on Heaven's Door: Artificial Intelligence as a Digital Architecture of Memory and Farewell

**Author:** Levent Can Ceylan (Experience Designer and Philosophical Lead)  
**Project Team Members:** Ömer Faruk Önder, Şükran Hilal Hocaoğlu, Levent Can Ceylan  

## 1. Introduction: The Epistemology of Removing the Badge

In the iconic 1973 film *Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid*, directed by Sam Peckinpah, there exists a profound sequence that transcends the traditional Western genre. An aging sheriff, fatally wounded, stumbles to the edge of a riverbank. Knowing his end is imminent, he slowly removes his bloodstained sheriff's badge and hands it to his weeping wife. "Take this," he whispers, "I can't use it anymore. It's getting dark, too dark to see. Feel like I'm knockin' on heaven's door." At that exact moment, Bob Dylan's immortal ballad begins to play. This cinematic sequence is not merely the depiction of a dying man; it is the visual encapsulation of abandoning a burden, shedding an identity, and the existential weight of a final farewell. The sheriff's badge is a metaphor for systemic responsibility, moral fatigue, and the temporal nature of human endeavors. 

In contemporary existence, we no longer wear literal tin stars or patrol dusty frontiers. Yet, the metaphorical act of "removing the badge" remains a universal human condition. When we resign from a career that defined us, when we migrate from a city that shaped our youth, when we dissolve a long-term relationship, or when we abandon a deeply held ideological belief, we are participating in the exact same ritual. We are shedding an identity. We are leaving a part of our history behind. However, the modern world—characterized by hyper-connectivity and ephemeral digital footprints—offers little space for the solemnity of such farewells. We delete a file, we change a relationship status, we clear a browser cache, and the mourning period is abruptly concluded. 

*KnockProject* emerges as a digital counter-narrative to this modern amnesia. It proposes an architectural framework for memory, utilizing Artificial Intelligence not as a computational oracle, but as a collaborative archivist. By creating a digital "Purgatory" or a "Memory Wall," the project allows users to input their modern, personal farewells, and in return, it cross-references these intimate moments with the collective historical sorrow of 1973. Through the integration of a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture and generative visual models, we transform a fleeting user input into a permanent, philosophical "epigraph" and a visual artifact—a rusted, personalized sheriff's badge.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Why 1973?

The selection of the year 1973 as our contextual baseline is a deliberate philosophical choice, grounded in the observation that certain historical periods crystallize collective disillusionment in ways that later epochs rarely replicate. The year stands as an epochal threshold in the 20th century: the final withdrawal of American forces from Vietnam following the Paris Peace Accords, the eruption of the Watergate scandal that would eventually dismantle a sitting presidency, the oil embargo imposed by OPEC that shattered Western economic certainty, and the Chilean coup that overthrew Salvador Allende—each event contributing to a pervasive atmosphere of institutional betrayal and moral exhaustion. It was a time when the world itself was collectively "removing its badge."

From a cultural-theoretical standpoint, 1973 occupies what Raymond Williams would describe as a "structure of feeling"—an emergent sensibility that had not yet been codified into formal ideology but was palpable in everyday discourse, artistic production, and personal correspondence. The anti-war protest letters, the combat diaries mailed home from Southeast Asia, and the folk-rock lyricism of artists like Bob Dylan and Leonard Cohen collectively constitute what Walter Benjamin might have termed a "dialectical image": a constellation of historical fragments that, when juxtaposed with present experience, illuminates the continuities of human suffering across temporal boundaries.

When a contemporary user submits a farewell statement such as, "I am finally leaving the company I built; I am exhausted but I will miss the team," a generic AI response would offer a sterile platitude. However, our system intercepts this input and dives into a curated vector database of 1973 artifacts: anti-war protest letters, combat diaries from Vietnam, melancholic poetry of the era, and the lyricism of Bob Dylan. Through semantic similarity search (Cosine Similarity), the system retrieves the historical echo of the user's modern pain. The resulting generated text is not an artificial fabrication from a vacuum, but a synthesis of a present-day departure and a historical sorrow. This methodology achieves what Carl Jung described as tapping into the "collective unconscious." The user realizes that their individual exhaustion or grief is not isolated; it is inextricably linked to a timeless human continuum. 1973 serves as the perfect historical mirror for modern fatigue.

## 3. The Ontological Status of AI in the Creative Process: Tool, Collaborator, or Mirror?

Throughout the development of *KnockProject*, our most rigorous intellectual debates centered on the ontological status of Artificial Intelligence within our creative workflow. It was imperative that we defined our relationship with the machine, not merely as an engineering decision, but as a philosophical stance that would shape every subsequent design choice.

**3.1. AI as a Tool**  
At its foundational layer, AI functions undeniably as a tool. The PostgreSQL database configured with the `pgvector` extension, the API endpoints built on .NET 8, and the underlying HTTP client architectures represent the mechanical scaffolding of the project. In this capacity, the AI is passive; it executes the semantic searches and processes the computational load of vector embeddings based on our exact instructions. It operates under deterministic principles, sorting data with mathematical precision. This instrumental understanding of AI aligns with Martin Heidegger's concept of *Zuhandenheit* ("readiness-to-hand"), wherein a tool recedes from conscious attention as long as it functions reliably—the database query engine disappears into the background of the creative act, just as a hammer disappears in the hands of a carpenter.

**3.2. AI as a Collaborator**  
However, when we enter the realm of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Prompt Engineering, the AI transcends the definition of a mere tool and becomes a "collaborator." By establishing strict boundary conditions—instructing the model to adopt the persona of a melancholic 1973 poet—we engaged in a co-authoring process. The AI provided literary nuances, unpredictable linguistic turns, and a depth of poetic synthesis that exceeded the rigid parameters of traditional programming. Similarly, when instructing generative visual models with rigid aesthetic constraints—"1973 analog film photography, rusty metal texture, surreal sheriff badge"—the AI interpreted these constraints to produce visual artifacts that possessed an unexpected, haunting beauty. The machine was not just following orders; it was interpreting a mood. This collaborative dimension raises questions about authorship and intentionality that remain unresolved in contemporary aesthetics, yet our practical experience confirmed that the most compelling outputs emerged precisely at the boundary between human directive and machine interpretation.

**3.3. AI as a Mirror**  
Ultimately, the most profound realization was that the AI served as a "mirror." When the system receives a mundane human input and reflects it back accompanied by a 50-year-old historical sorrow, it acts as a reflective surface for human empathy. The machine itself feels nothing, but by associating our modern text with the archived pain of 1973, it forces the user to confront their own humanity. The AI becomes a medium through which we observe our own universal vulnerability. It does not generate emotion; it reflects the emotion we have embedded within our historical data. In this sense, it fulfills a function analogous to what Susan Sontag described when discussing photography's capacity to "furnish evidence"—the AI furnishes evidence of emotional kinship across decades, compelling the viewer to acknowledge a continuity they might otherwise deny.

## 4. Experience Design and Interface Aesthetics

As the Experience Designer, my primary objective was to ensure that the user's interaction with the system felt less like operating a web application and more like participating in a solemn ritual. The interface (UI) was designed to represent the concept of "Knocking on Heaven's Door"—a threshold between the past and the present.

The aesthetic paradigm strictly adheres to a minimalist, dark, 1973 analog visual language. We eliminated all superfluous modern web components: no vibrant color palettes, no distracting animations, and no commercial signifiers. The interface features a dark background overlaid with subtle film grain (noise), invoking the tactile quality of vintage cinema. The typography relies on monospaced typewriter fonts, reminiscent of redacted military documents or underground press publications of the 1970s.

When the user types their farewell into the central text area and clicks the "Leave the Badge" button, the system initiates a complex orchestration of API calls, vector searches, and LLM text generation. However, the user is shielded from this technological complexity. They are presented with a real-time loading state—powered by a SignalR synchronization layer—that communicates the machine's internal processing as a series of ritualistic steps: *"Embedding the departure," "Searching for historical echoes," "Composing the epigraph."* 

This intentional friction—a deliberate waiting period—simulates the psychological weight of reflection. The design philosophy draws from the tradition of "Slow Technology," where temporal delay is reintroduced to foster mindfulness. This is further reinforced by the **Typewriter (Daktilo) Engine**: the generated epigraph does not appear instantly but is typed out character by character. This rhythmic, percussive visual feedback mimics the analog labor of the 1970s, forcing the user to read at the speed of human thought rather than the speed of fiber-optic processing.

### 4.1. Auditory Synthesis: The 1973 Soundscape

A profound expansion of *KnockProject* is its integration of the auditory dimension. 1973 was not just a year of images and letters; it was a year of a specific sonic texture. By integrating a context-aware YouTube Music layer, the system analyzes the emotional frequency of the user’s veda and pairs it with a historically accurate track—be it the weary strings of Pink Floyd, the defiant folk of Joan Baez, or the melancholic blues of the era. This creates a "synesthetic closure," where the user’s personal departure is synchronized with the actual soundtrack of 1973. The music serves as the emotional glue, transforming the digital interaction into a cinematic farewell.

### 4.2. The Digital Relic: Preservation of the Moment

In the final stage of the experience, the system allows the user to download a generated "Digital Postcard." This artifact—containing the rusted badge and the typed epigraph—functions as a *digital relic*. In an era of ephemeral data, giving the user a permanent image to keep acts as a modern equivalent to the sheriff handing over his physical badge. It is a "monument in the pocket," a small, persistent reminder that the farewell has been acknowledged by both the past and the machine.

## 6. Ethical Considerations and Methodological Constraints

No project that situates itself at the intersection of historical memory and generative AI can afford to ignore its ethical obligations. We confronted several methodological tensions that merit transparent disclosure.

First, the question of historical fidelity: our curated dataset inevitably reflects editorial choices. The Vietnam-era letters and protest documents we selected represent a particular strand of 1973 discourse—predominantly anglophone, predominantly anti-establishment. We acknowledge that this curatorial lens shapes the emotional register of the system's outputs and does not claim to represent the totality of that year's human experience. Future iterations of the project could benefit from multilingual source material and perspectives from regions beyond the North American and Western European sphere.

Second, the issue of emotional responsibility: by design, *KnockProject* engages users at moments of personal vulnerability. A person composing a farewell is, almost by definition, in a state of transition or distress. We took care to ensure that the system's outputs never trivialize the user's input, never redirect it toward commercial ends, and never store personally identifiable information beyond the duration of a single session. The ephemeral nature of the interaction—no user data is persisted—was a deliberate architectural choice rooted in respect for the intimacy of the act.

Third, the challenge of generative authenticity: Large Language Models are prone to what researchers colloquially term "hallucination"—the production of plausible but fabricated content. Within our context, this risk manifests as the potential for the AI to generate pseudo-historical references that sound authentic but have no factual grounding. We mitigated this through the RAG pipeline, which grounds the model's generation in verified, pre-curated documents rather than allowing unconstrained synthesis. The retrieval step acts as an epistemic anchor, tethering the model's creative latitude to the material reality of our 1973 archive.

## 7. Conclusion: The Perpetuity of the Echo

*KnockProject* is an exploration of how advanced computational models can be repurposed to serve emotional and historical archiving. By bridging the gap between a modern personal farewell and the collective melancholia of 1973, we have created a system that honors closure. We have demonstrated that Artificial Intelligence, when guided by a strong philosophical framework and rigorous artistic constraints, can transcend its role as a productivity tool and become an instrument of empathy.

The project does not claim to resolve the tensions it raises—between human authorship and machine generation, between historical truth and curated narrative, between technological mediation and genuine emotional experience. Rather, it holds these tensions in productive suspension, inviting both the user and the critical observer to sit with the discomfort and to ask whether the echo the machine produces is any less meaningful for having passed through silicon before reaching the ear.

As we navigate the complexities of modern life, we will inevitably accumulate burdens, and we will inevitably need to leave them behind. We invite you to use this digital architecture to lay down your burdens. 

Leave your badge at the door. The river keeps flowing without you, but your echo will remain.
